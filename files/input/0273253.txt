The mundane pattern of performing the same routine tasks and seeing nothing in return but a paycheck. Being a cog in the machine. I'm a fan of Office Space but like most mainstream comedies it's a mindless work that aims for the lowest common denominator. With Office Space what you see is what you get. This is not the case with Haiku Tunnel. Like most great stories there is a plot on the surface but the real story lies beneath. On the surface Haiku Tunnel is a movie about the tedious life of working in an office, specifically viewed through the eyes of Josh Kornbluth, a man who works for a temp agency. If someone watched the film to follow the plot and see what happened next I can see why they hated it. It's my opinion that the people who expected this movie to be like Office Space were thoroughly disappointed and gave the film a poor review. Although there is a plot this is not a plot driven movie. That's where it differs from Office Space.Haiku Tunnel is a film about a man's struggle to find his place in the world. He is trying to find balance in his life and is uncertain about just about everything, mainly his inability to commit. Once Josh gets to a point where he disconnects from other people as much as humanly possible he finds he is miserable. Yet when he gets the chance to commit to anything he either refuses or does so kicking and screaming. Such is the case when he sabotages himself by not completing the task he is assigned once he is talked into becoming a permanent employee. Josh clearly has an internal conflict. On one level he wants to be accepted and be part of something. He wants stability and human interaction. But on another level part of him realizes with this comes responsibility and he is terrified by the idea of taking on responsibility. He's fighting the very thought of becoming a cog in the machine. This transcends his job and describes his life as a whole. He has spent his whole life wandering aimlessly from one thing to the next managing to avoid taking on any significant responsibility. His inability to complete the simple task at work can be taken as a microcosm of his life as a whole. It can be argued that his novel is just a bigger task that is destined to never be completed. By having his novel to fall back on Josh can avoid having a life by continuously writing while avoiding any hint of a life. Thus the office becomes an analogy for life itself and the film asks some significant questions. What is your objective in life? What is your role in society? Do you aspire to accomplish things or are you content just being? What about the world frightens you? Why? What can you do about it? Do you care? I found the plot on the surface to be entertaining and the characters to be funny and interesting. There is something both endearing and terrifying about Josh Kornbluth. Throughout the film I wasn't really sure if I wanted him to succeed. I'm still not sure if I like him. And I like that. It's an added challenge the film provides. In order to truly appreciate this film you don't have to have experience working in an office environment. What it takes to appreciate this film is a strong degree of empathy. The ability to put yourself in Josh's shoes, both on a professional and personal level and contemplate the issues he is dealing with. If you are able to do that I feel you will enjoy this movie. However, if you're looking to sit back, turn off your brain and laugh out loud watch Office Space. Either way you can't go wrong.
